Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

Consider the function: \[ p(t) = \|t \vec{y} - \vec{x}\|^2 \]

Since it represents a squared norm, it must be non-negative: \[ p(t) = \|t \vec{y} - \vec{x}\|^2 \geq 0 \]

Expanding using the dot product: \[ p(t) = (t \vec{y} - \vec{x}) \cdot (t \vec{y} - \vec{x}) \geq 0 \] \[ p(t) = t^2 (\vec{y} \cdot \vec{y}) - 2t (\vec{x} \cdot \vec{y}) + (\vec{x} \cdot \vec{x}) \geq 0 \]

Defining coefficients for simplicity: \[ p(t) = at^2 + bt + c \geq 0 \] where: \[ a = (\vec{y} \cdot \vec{y}), \quad b = -2 (\vec{x} \cdot \vec{y}), \quad c = (\vec{x} \cdot \vec{x}) \]

The minimum of this quadratic function occurs at: \[ t = -\frac{b}{2a} \]

Substituting \( t \) back into \( p(t) \): \[ p\left(-\frac{b}{2a}\right) = \frac{b^2}{4a} - \frac{b^2}{2a} + c \geq 0 \]

Simplifying: \[ 4ac \geq b^2 \]

Rewriting in terms of the original dot products: \[ 4 (\vec{y} \cdot \vec{y}) (\vec{x} \cdot \vec{x}) \geq (2 \vec{x} \cdot \vec{y})^2 \]

Using the identity \( \vec{v} \cdot \vec{v} = \|\vec{v}\|^2 \): \[ 4 \|\vec{y}\|^2 \|\vec{x}\|^2 \geq (|-2 \vec{x} \cdot \vec{y}|)^2 \]

Taking the square root on both sides: \[ 2 \|\vec{y}\| \|\vec{x}\| \geq 2 |\vec{x} \cdot \vec{y}| \]

Simplifying: \[ \|\vec{y}\| \|\vec{x}\| \geq |\vec{x} \cdot \vec{y}| \]

This proves the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality.

Independence vs. Uncorrelation

Independence and uncorrelation are distinct concepts in probability and statistics.

  1. Independence: Two random variables \( X \) and \( Y \) are independent if: \[ P(X \cap Y) = P(X) P(Y) \] This means knowing one does not affect the probability distribution of the other.
  2. Uncorrelation: Two variables \( X \) and \( Y \) are uncorrelated if their covariance is zero: \[ \operatorname{Cov}(X, Y) = \mathbb{E}[(X - \mathbb{E}[X])(Y - \mathbb{E}[Y])] = 0 \] This implies no linear relationship but does not rule out nonlinear dependence.

Key Differences

  • Scope: Independence ensures no dependency at all, while uncorrelation only eliminates linear dependence.
  • Implications: Independence implies uncorrelation, but the reverse is not necessarily true. For example, if \( Y = X^2 \), then \( X \) and \( Y \) can be uncorrelated but dependent.

Measuring Dependence

  • Correlation Coefficient: The correlation coefficient: \[ \rho_{X,Y} = \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X, Y)}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y} \] measures linear dependence, but a zero value does not imply independence.
  • Mutual Information: Unlike correlation, mutual information captures all forms of dependence. If mutual information is zero, the variables are truly independent.

In summary, while uncorrelation implies no linear dependence, independence is a stronger condition requiring that the variables have no dependency at all.
